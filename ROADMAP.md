## Roadmap Philosophy

> Don't Memorize. **Understand.**
> Don't Rush. **Master.**
> Don't Count Questions. **Understand Patterns.**
> Don't Watch Videos Forever. **Write Code.**

The single biggest failure mode for beginners is passive learning — watching tutorial after tutorial and feeling like you're learning, without ever struggling with a blank editor. This roadmap is built to force **active learning**: you read a concept once, then you spend the rest of the day proving to yourself that you understand it by writing code, breaking it, and fixing it.

---

## Rules to Follow

**Rule 1 — Never copy code.**
Type every line yourself, even when copying from a solution. Muscle memory matters.

**Rule 2 — Always write brute force first.**
Never jump to the optimal solution. Understand the naive approach before optimizing it.

**Rule 3 — Always analyze Time Complexity.**
Every solution you write must end with a Big O analysis, written in your own words.

**Rule 4 — Spend at least 30 minutes thinking before checking a solution.**
The struggle is where the learning happens. Do not skip it.

**Rule 5 — Never skip revision.**
Unrevised knowledge decays. Follow the [Revision Cycle](#revision-strategy) without exception.

**Rule 6 — Write notes.**
For every topic, write a short markdown note in your own words in the corresponding day's folder.

**Rule 7 — Maintain a Mistake Notebook.**
Every time you get something wrong, log it: what you did, why it was wrong, what the fix was.

**Rule 8 — Teach others.**
Explain what you learned to someone else — a friend, a rubber duck, or a README. If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it yet.

**Rule 9 — Consistency beats intensity.**
One hour daily for 120 days beats a 12-hour binge once a week. Never break the streak.

**Rule 10 — Code every single day.**
No zero days. Even on your worst day, write at least one line of code.

## Learning Methodology

For every new topic in this roadmap, follow this exact sequence:

1. **Read** — Understand the concept from one primary resource (see [Resources](#resources)). Do not jump between five different explanations.
2. **Visualize** — Draw it. Boxes, arrows, dry runs on paper. If you can't draw it, you don't understand it.
3. **Implement from scratch** — Write the data structure or algorithm yourself, without looking at a reference implementation.
4. **Solve** — Apply it to 5–10 problems, starting Easy, moving to Medium.
5. **Explain** — Write a short note explaining the concept as if teaching a beginner.
6. **Log mistakes** — Anything you got wrong goes into the Mistake Notebook.
7. **Revise** — Follow the spaced revision cycle on Day 1, 3, 7, 15, and 30 after first learning it.

## Problem Solving Strategy

Use this exact sequence for every problem, in interviews and practice alike:

```
Understand → Observe → Brute Force → Optimize →
Code → Dry Run → Complexity → Edge Cases → Submit → Revise
```

1. **Understand** — Restate the problem in your own words. Ask clarifying questions.
2. **Observe** — Identify constraints, patterns, and edge cases from the input size and rules.
3. **Brute Force** — Write the simplest correct solution, even if slow.
4. **Optimize** — Identify the bottleneck and apply the right pattern (two pointer, hashing, DP, etc.).
5. **Code** — Implement cleanly, with meaningful variable names.
6. **Dry Run** — Trace through at least one example by hand before running it.
7. **Complexity** — State time and space complexity out loud.
8. **Edge Cases** — Empty input, single element, duplicates, negative numbers, overflow.
9. **Submit** — Run against test cases.
10. **Revise** — Log it in your Mistake Notebook if anything went wrong.

## Revision Strategy

Spaced repetition, applied to every topic and every problem set:

```
Day 1 → Day 3 → Day 7 → Day 15 → Day 30 → Interview Week
```

- **Day 1:** Learn the topic.
- **Day 3:** Re-solve 2–3 problems from that topic without notes.
- **Day 7:** Re-solve 2–3 different problems from that topic.
- **Day 15:** Skim your notes, re-solve 1 hard problem from that topic.
- **Day 30:** Full topic review — could you teach it in 10 minutes?
- **Interview Week:** Rapid-fire review of every topic's core pattern and one representative problem.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I have zero coding background. Will I really be able to follow this?**
Yes. Phase 1 assumes literally zero prior knowledge and builds up from "what is a variable."

**Q: What if I can't dedicate 6–8 hours a day?**
Stretch the timeline proportionally. 3 hours/day roughly doubles the duration to ~240 days. Never skip content — only slow down.

**Q: Should I use a different language instead of Python?**
This roadmap is Python-specific by design — its simple syntax lets you focus on logic, not language quirks. You can transfer these concepts to any language later.

**Q: What if I fall behind schedule?**
Falling behind is fine. Skipping revision is not. Adjust the day count, but never skip Rule 5.

**Q: Do I need to solve every single problem on every topic?**
No — this roadmap gives you a pattern-based approach. Once you can recognize and solve a pattern confidently in 3–5 problems, move on. Depth over quantity.

**Q: What if I get stuck on a problem for hours?**
Follow Rule 4 — think for at least 30 minutes. After that, look at a hint (not the full solution), attempt again, and only view the full solution if still stuck. Always log it in your Mistake Notebook.

**Q: Is 120 days enough to get into a product-based company?**
It's enough to become interview-ready in DSA. Actual placement also depends on applications, resume, referrals, and mock interview performance — all covered in Phase 6.
